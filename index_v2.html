Spectrograms

What is a Spectrogram?

A spectrogram is a visual representation of sound that allows you to see how audio evolves over time. Rather than relying only on listening, it translates sound into patterns of frequency and energy, making invisible acoustic details immediately observable.

Every sound is made up of multiple frequencies layered together. When we speak, walk, or interact with our environment, we produce complex combinations of tones, bursts, and textures. A spectrogram captures all of these components simultaneously, revealing structure that is often difficult to detect by ear alone.

Because of this, spectrograms are widely used in fields like speech analysis, medicine, linguistics, and audio engineering. They make it possible to identify patterns such as rhythm, clarity, consistency, and irregularities in sound, features that can be critical when analyzing behavior or health.

How to Read a Spectrogram

  • Time (horizontal axis):
    Moves from left to right, showing when sounds occur and how long they last.
  • Frequency (vertical axis):
    Represents pitch. Lower frequencies (like vowels or hums) appear toward the bottom, while higher frequencies (like "s", "f", or sharp noises) appear toward the top.
  • Intensity (color or darkness):
    Indicates how strong a sound is. Darker regions mean more energy (louder or more prominent sounds), while lighter regions indicate weaker sounds.

What You Can Learn From It

A spectrogram does not just show sound, it helps you analyze it:

  • Timing and rhythm:
    You can see pauses, repetition, and pacing (for example, steady walking vs. irregular movement).
  • Clarity and articulation:
    In speech, well-formed sounds create clear, structured patterns, while unclear or slurred speech appears more diffuse or irregular.
  • Consistency vs. variability:
    Repeating patterns suggest stable behavior, while sudden spikes or disruptions may indicate changes in environment or condition.
  • Type of sound source:
    Continuous sounds (like rain or humming) create smooth, consistent textures, while abrupt sounds (like footsteps or barking) appear as distinct bursts.

Key Vocabulary

  • Frequency (Hz):
    The number of vibrations per second in a sound wave. Higher frequency sounds are higher in pitch, while lower frequency sounds are deeper.
  • Pitch:
    How high or low a sound is perceived. Pitch is closely related to frequency but describes how we hear that frequency.
  • Amplitude (Energy):
    The strength or loudness of a sound. In a spectrogram, this is shown by color intensity, darker areas indicate stronger sound energy.
  • Harmonics:
    Multiple frequencies that occur together in structured sounds like speech or music. These often appear as horizontal bands and help define voice quality.
  • Noise:
    Random or unstructured sound energy (like static, rain, or background noise). It appears more scattered and less organized than speech patterns.

Background noise, like rain, typing, and distant chatter, can make a spectrogram harder to read.
